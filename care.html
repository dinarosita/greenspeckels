Green Speckles: Care

Watering

This section is not maintained anymore. Go to:

Water Log

High: Don't let soil dry out.

  • African Violet
    • Warm water
    • January 27. February fed-5, e15, fed-27
    • 400-518 g
    • Warm water is used since 15 Feb.
    • Keep soil moist with warm water. Don't allow water to have contact with leaves other than light misting. Water from below. Don't let the plants sit in water.
  • Arugula
    • January 27. February 5, fed-15, 24
    • 1530-2355 g
    • Regular watering. Water as soon as top inch feels dry. Keep soil consistently moist but not soggy.
    • Fail to do this might cause plant to bolt and flavor ruined.
  • Basil
    • Big basil: January 27. February fed-3, 14, 27. Mar 3-fed
    • Big basil: 250-320 g
    • Basil babies: January 27. February fed-8, 20, Mar 4-fed
    • Basil babies: 1560-2350 g
    • Keep soil regularly moist, 1" of water a week for outdoor, bit more for containers. Water once the top layer of soil dries out, definitely when plant show first sign of wilting (don't let it happen! don't wait for this signal!).
  • Chili
    • Big chili: February fed-2, 8, fed-15. Mar 3-fed
    • Chili babies: January 27. February fed-3, 11, 15, fed-20, 27
    • 2480-3100 g
    • Keep soil moist at all times, never allow soil to become bone dry as it will cause the blossom to drop. Especially during hot dry days. Containers might even need to be watered as frequent as twice a day if it dries out quickly.
  • Cilantro
    • Sow Mar 3.
    • 1300-2000 g
    • Keep regularly moist but not soaked. Good drainage is important because cilantro has deep root. 1" per week.
  • Coleus
    • January 27. February 11, fed-24
    • Consistenly moist but not soggy. It should not wet all the time, but too long dry will slow plant growth. For indoor, water maybe every 2-3 days, or more if climate rather dry.
  • Fern
    • January 27. February 18.March fed-1 100ml
    • 360-440 g
    • Keep moist, don't let completely dry out. Let water run through the drainage, then wait until top soil looking a bit drier before the next watering.
  • Kemangi
    • January 27. February 5, fed-15. March fed-1
    • 1425-2200 g
    • Moderate level of water. Consistently moist soil is best, don't overwater, don't let dry out.
  • Parsley
    • January 27. February fed-4, 11, 15, 20, 24, 27. Mar 4
    • 220-360 g
    • Keep evenly moist but not soggy. 1-2" water/week. Never let the soil to dry out, parsley doeesn't tolerate drought well, will quickly wither and brown.
  • Polka dot plant
    • January 27. February fed-3, 11, 20, Mar 2-fed
    • 310-450 g
    • Moderate amount of moisture in soil at all time. Water when top hald inch dried out. Don't let soil completely dried out. It can lead foliage to wilt and struggling to survive. Don't get it soggy either, it can cause root rot and kill the plant.
  • Potato
    • January 27. February fed-3 Feb, 11, fed-15, fed-20, pot-22
    • 2844-3760 g
    • Rely on steady water supply. Water at least 1 inch a week. Sensitive to drought especially when flowers (which is the peak time to form tubers).
  • Turmeric
    • January 27. February 9 fed-27
    • ...-1885 g
    • tolerate wet soil, keep moist, don't let soil dry out.

Moderate: Let the soil dry a little bit between watering

  • Alyssum
    • January 27. February 11, fed-15
    • At least an inch a week. More if the air is dry. Drain well, susceptible to root rot. Water deeply and allow the potting mix to dry slightly before watering again.
  • Candytuft
    • January 27. February 5, 15, 27-fed
    • 775-980 g
    • Once established, moderately drought-tolerant. But make sure to water young plants, especially during dry period.
    • For young plants: Water twice a week. Once established: water once a week.
  • Ellwood Cypress
    • January 27. February fed-8
    • The youngs like moderate amount of water, but dont like wet feet, susceptible to root rot and often fatal. Water well at least once a week especially in dry weather. Once established watering can be reduced
  • English ivy
    • January 27. February fed-8, 20
    • 344-630 g
    • Prefers slightly on the dry side. Prefers moist but not soggy soil. Let the soil dry out some (dry to the touch on top) before the next watering.
  • Poinsettia
    • January 27. February 6, 15
    • Water when the soil surface is dry to touch. Saturate the soil completely and let water drain. Overwatering can kill the plant, with wilting leaves as the sign.
  • Rosemary
    • January 27. February 11, fed-15, 20, 27. Mar 4
    • 200-260 g
    • Water when the top of the soil has dried out, but never let all the soil dry out completely.
    • "Upside down" plant. Prefers to absorb moisture through air. Gently misting the plant every 10 days to fill the gap between watering.
  • Sage
    • January 27. February 5 Feb, fed-8, 18, 24
    • 1288-1662 g.
    • Moderate moisture need, some drought tolerance.
    • For young plants: keep soil evently moist but not soggy.
    • For established plants: Water when top 1-2 inches of soil dries out.
    • Avoid getting the leaves wet. Can cause mildew.
  • Scarlet Flax
    • January 27. February 8, 18, Mar 2, 4
    • 1550-1740 g
    • Low to moderate. Overwatering can cause root rot. Very hardly and drought tolerante. But it also says, keep soil moist, water occasionally.
  • Soapwort
    • January 27. February fed-8, 27
    • 1680-2080 g
    • Prefers consistent moisture but can tolerate a few days of drought once established. Soak the soil well, let soil dry to touch but not completely through before the next watering.
  • String of turtles
    • January 27. February 11, fed-24
    • Prefers slightly moist, however more prone to suffer from overwatering than underwatering. Water thoroughly to get the soil evenly moist, let the soil dry out between watering. Don't water again until top 2 inches of soil have dried out.

Low

  • Bunny tails
    • January 27. February 6, 14, 20, 24, 27. Mar 3
    • 1410-1850 g
    • Very forgiving, drought resistant. Water deeply and let it dry out before the next watering. Doesn't like wet feet and root might rot if constantly wet.
  • Dianthus
    • January 27. February 5, 11, 18, fed-24, Mar 2
    • 1525-1750 g
    • Low to average. Water 1 inch weekly. Drought tolerance.
  • Lavender
    • January 27. February 5, 15
    • wet 2579
    • Water sparingly. Allow to dry out between watering, but don't let the plant wilts. Drought-tolerant once established. Too much water can lead to fungal disease and root rot. For first growing season though, water regularly.
  • Oregano
    • January 27. February 11, 18
    • 258 wet
    • Water regularly but not excessively. Let soil dry completely between waterings. drought resistant.
  • Thyme
    • January 27. February 11, fed-24
    • Drought resistant, prefer under-watered than over-watered. Wait soil to be completely dry before watering. Flower is not a sign of bolting or over-watering. It will continue to thrive as long as keep trimming to promote new growth.

Succulent

  • Haworthia
    • January 27. February 8, fed-18
    • 1700-2110 g
    • In spring and summer months, water whenever top inch soil has dried out. In fall and winter: reduce watering to just enough to keep the leaves plump. Don't let water collect in the rosette, this can lead to rot.
  • Harry's cactus
    • February fed-18
    • 315-420 g
  • Livvy's cactus
    • February fed-18
    • 425-465 g
  • Peperomia green beans
    • February fed-18
    • 350-550 g
    • Wilting when underwatered, rotting when overwatered. Water every 7-10 days, but this depend on the humidity. Water the potting mix thoroughly, drain well. Make sure soil is dry before the next watering.

Water Log

High: once or more each month, even though might need winter break.

  • African Violet
    • Fertilizer: rose ¼
    • Dates: February 5, 27
    • Feed biweekly with an African violet fertilizer.
  • Chili
    • Fertilizer: all purpose ½ (non-flowering), fruit and veg ½ (flowering), or mixed
    • Big chili: January 15. February 2, 15, Mar 3
    • Chili babies: February 3, 20
    • Maybe every 2 weeks for big chili.
    • Fertilize immediately after planting using all-purpose granular fertilizer, fertilize again when small peppers begin to form. For containers, use all-purpose balanced liquid fertilizer.
    • Don't overdo nitrogen, it can deter fruit growth. Should be rich in phosporus, potassium, and calcium.
  • Cilantro
    • Fertilizer: all purpose ½
    • Dates:
    • Use liquid fertilizer, feed once a month.
  • English ivy
    • Fertilizer: all purpose ½
    • Dates: February 8
    • Every 2 weeks during spring and summer seasons using 20-20-20. Don't fertilize if the plant in stressful situation: very hot, very cold, or very dry soil, or when there's no leaf production.
  • Oregano
    • Fertilizer: all purpose ½
    • Dates: NEXT WATERING
    • Every 2 weeks with diluted liquid fertilizer.
  • Peperomia green bean
    • Fertilizer: all purpose ½ (was using rose ⅓ first feeding
    • Dates: February 18
    • In summer, feed every third watering with balanced liquid plant food. In other season, feed monthly.
  • Polka dot plant
    • Fertilizer: all purpose ½
    • Dates: February 3, mar 2
    • Heavy feeder. Once a month during warm growing season.
  • Potato
    • Fertilizer: all purpose ½
    • Dates: February 3, 15, 20
    • Every 2 weeks with diluted liquid fertilizer or fish emulsion.
  • Rosemary
    • Fertilizer: all purpose ½
    • Dates: February 15
    • Feed with liquid fertilizer at the start of growing season. Continuing monthly through the fall.
  • String of turtles
    • Fertilizer: all purpose ½
    • Dates: 24
    • Feeding the plant can help the plant maintain its bright shiny vigor and its color and pattern too. Feed with all-purpose fertilizer biweekly during the growing season,but not in fall and winter season.
  • Turmeric
    • Fertilizer: all purpose ½
    • Dates: Feb 27
    • Needs a lot of nutrient. Feed every month. All purpose liquid fertilizer works best.

Medium: As needed, or every several months

  • Alyssum
    • Fertilizer: all purpose ½
    • Dates: February 15
    • White and yellow alyssum have different fertilizing requirement. For white alyssum in container, feed monthly with water-soluble well balanced fertilizer. Slow release fertilizer when planting and after bloom spent. For yellow one, unless the soil very lean, doesn't need fertilizing. Too much fertilizer can cause poor flowering and scraggly growth.
  • Basil
    • Fertilizer: all purpose ½
    • Big basil: February 3, Mar 3
    • Basil babies: Mar 4
    • If planted in organic compost mixture, maybe don't need more nutrient. If plants not growing much, feed once a month with weak liquid fertilizer.
  • Candytuft
    • Fertilizer: rose ½
    • Dates: Feb 27-fed
    • Not necessary but can make bloom more abundant. Low nitrogen, high phosporous. Miracle gro bloom formula is 15-30-15, probably best choice, followed by rose at 18-24-16. Slow releasing fertilizer is a good idea when planting. Otherwise, when leaves start forming, then every a half year. Or even just once a year, in early spring.
  • Kemangi
    • Fertilizer: all purpose ½
    • Dates: February 15, Mar 1
    • In one reference: 2-4 times a year.
    • In another reference: moderate to high level of nutrients, Feed every 2-3 weeks during warm growing season.
  • Parsley
    • Fertilizer: all purpose ½
    • Dates: February 4
    • Once or twice in the beginning of growing season. Once a month with half strength balanced organic liquid fertilizer.

Low

  • Arugula
    • Fertilizer: all purpose ½
    • Dates: February 15
    • If planted in nitrogen rich soil, doesn't need additional feeding. If it's lack of nourishment, the leaves turn pale.
  • Coleus
    • Fertilizer: all purpose ½
    • Dates: February 24

Container ones might benefit from slow-released fertilizer when starting the pot. Thereafter, feed with diluted liquid fertilizer every 1-2 weeks. Container plants generally need more frequent feeding than garden plants because frequent watering washes the nutrients away.
  • Dianthus

  • Ellwood Cypress
    • Fertilizer: all purpose ½
    • Dates: February 8
    • Doesn't need fertilizer unless the soil is known to be nutrient poor. In such case, use all purpose before new spring growth begin.
  • Fern
    • Fertilizer: all purpose ½
    • Dates: Mar 1
    • Fertilizer: Balanced 10-10-10, half strength every watering, or use it every 2 weeks.
  • Sage
    • Fertilizer: all purpose ½
    • Dates: February 8
    • Not heavy feeder. Too muc hfertilizer can weaken flavour.
  • Soapwort
    • Fertilizer: all purpose ½
    • Dates: February 8
    • Once established, can grow for years wihout feeding.
    • For poor soil, feed annualy with all purpose fertilizer. Feed lightly only because too many nutrient can damage the plant and hinder its growth
  • Thyme

    • Dates: 24

Sunlight

Full sun

  • Alyssum
    • Full sun, but doesn't like prolonged dry period. 6-8 hours of sun a day.
  • Arugula
    • Full sun. But if temperature is high, provide shading to avoid wilting and bolting.
  • Basil
    • Full sun.
  • Candytuft
    • Like full sun but will tolerate some shade.
  • Chili
  • Dianthus
    • Full sun, 6 hours a day.
  • Ellwood Cypress
    • Put in direct sun - need to recover from browning tips.
    • Plenty of direct sunlight. Without enough sunlight, inner branches often die back. The tree can't produce new buds on old wood, so any brown or bare patches due to lack of light will not grow back.
  • Kemangi
    • 4-6 hours of full sun a day. Likes full sun, but tolerate partial shade. Will do just fine on a sunny windowsill.
  • Lavender
    • Thrive in full sunlight. 8 hours a day. Don't tolerate much shade.
  • Oregano
  • Parsley
    • Full sun.
  • Potato
    • Actually can handle part shade, but a lot of sunlight is needed to feed the tubers.
  • Rosemary
    • Full sunlight, at laest 6 hours a day. Only moderately tolerant to shade.
  • Sage
    • Full sun for the best flavor. 6 hours of direct sun a day.
  • Scarlet Flax
    • Grow very well in full sun, but will grow even in partial shade.
  • Soapwort
    • Full sun for best bloom. 6-8 hours of sunlight. Tolerate a little bit of shade.
  • Thyme
    • Full sun.
  • Turmeric
    • Full sun (north)

Partial shade to full sun

  • Bunny tails
    • Sow seeds in full sun, but established plants can grow well in partial shade too.
  • Haworthia
    • Slightly sheltered is better than full sunlight. It likes bright light but not strong direct sunlight. Tolerate morning sun, but not harsh all they long afternoon sunlight. It can burn its foliages. If the color is fading away though, that's the sign of not enough light. East or west facing window is the best.
  • Poinsettia
    • Bright difused sunlight. Near sunny window with 6-8 hours of light a day. Direct sunlight can burn bracts and leaves.

Medium

  • Cilantros
    • New location (both): Living room southeast window to give partial shade.
    • Likes bright indirect light, dislike intense, direct sunlight. Good options are east facing window for the morning sun, or part of very bright sill that doesn't get too much direct sun.

Low light or partial shade

  • Coleus
    • 13 Feb - Put in south window because just heavily pruned.
    • Part shade. For indoor containers, indirect sunlight in warm months, expose to filtered unlight during the winter. It does need some light even though just a little. If the plants get leggy and pinching doesn't help, it might need more sun.
  • English ivy
    • Part shade to full shade. For indoor: Bright indirect sunlight in summer, some direct light in winter.

Bright indirect sunlight

  • African violet
    • 8 Feb: move to southeast window every afternoon after the sun doesn't directly shine it.
    • Likes bright indirect room. Leaves can get burn under direct light, however still needs some sun to flower. Best is east facing window with sheer curtain. It does also need 8 hours of darkness at night.
  • English ivy
    • Bright indirect sunlight
  • Peperomia green bean
      Like bright filtered light. Can survive in low light but with delayed growth. Avoid direct sunlight.
  • Polka dot plant
    • Sign of too much sun is curling leaves and fading leaf color.
  • String of turtles
    • Current location: Living room, southeast window.
    • Thrives in bright indirect sunlight. Too long in full sun will damage the leaves. Too little sun will make it fail to produce new growth. Make sure sunlight reaching out all parts of the plant, not just the vining.

Indirect / dappled sunlight

Temperature

Warm - above 21°C

  • African Violet
    • Thrive at 21°C, don't let fall under 16°C.
  • Basil
    • Above 21°C.
  • Bunny tails
    • No specific mention on temperature found, but this plant is native to Mediterannean and summer loving. Even though partial shade is fine too.
  • Candytuft
    • Seeds need 24-30 °C to germinate, established plants can tolerate higher range. If left outside, when temperature drop, a little protection can help plants to surfive, like mulching.
  • Coleus
    • Like it hot. To put outside, wait until it's about 21°C.
  • Haworthia
    • Like 21-35°C of summer, and okay at around 10°C in the winter. Will suffer damage under 5°C
  • Kemangi
    • Like warm weather. Even like warm soil.
  • Peperomia
    • Like 18-24°C. Don't let the temperature to fall under 10°C.
  • Poinsettia
    • To keept it blooming as long as possible, keep the temperature between 18-24 °C during the day. Night can be colder.
    • Cold draft or letting the leaves touch the window can injure the leaves and cause premature yellowing and leaf drop.
  • Polka dot plant
    • 21-27°C
  • String of turtles
    • Keep it between 20-24 °C

Mild - above 16°C

  • Alyssum
    • White alyssum is not too picky, but the yellow one doesn't like it hot or too humid. Tend to die in the middle of summer heat. Often treated like annual because of this even though actually perennial.
  • Chili
  • Cilantros
    • 16-24°C. Bolted easily especially in warm weather. Once that happens, the flavors change, often become bitter. Only bring outside during spring and early fall, not during summer. Keep it in shaded area. When it gets too hot outside, bring inside to temperature about 21°C.
  • Potato
    • Soil temperature 16-21°C for best grow. Will stop growing at 27°C.
  • Sage
    • 16-21°C is best even though can withstand some frost.
  • Thyme
    • 16-27°C. To bring outoor, make sure temperature is above 16°C and choose sunny spot.

Cool - under 19°C

  • Arugula
    • 7-18°C
  • Parsley
    • 10-21°C

Wide range

  • Dianthus
    • Can stand wide temperature range.
    • In hot summer, if the temperature consistenly goes above 30°C, it will go dormant
    • Tolerate a light frost, don't like a deep freeze. Cover with frost blanket if temperature goes under 5°C
  • Ellwood cypress
    • 10-27 °C
  • English ivy
    • 7-27°C. Try to keep indoor ivy cool at night, about 15°C, if possible.
  • Lavender
    • Spruce said it can withstand wide range of temperature. But Gardening know how said it like heat and many varieties won't survive a cold winter.
  • Oregano
    • 10-27°C or even more.
  • Rosemary
      10-27°C. Even 4°C is probably okay.
  • Soapwort
    • Thrive in full range of conditions. Even survive under freezing temperature.

Harvest and Pruning

Ornamental leaves

  • Coleus
    • Thinning seedlings: 7 Feb, 12 Feb.
    • Pinching 27 feb
    • Pinch out the growing tips when plants are about 6" tall. If leaves preferred, pinch of flowers under the flower buds, so energy doesn't get drained by flowering and seeding process. Unpruned plants tends to get leggy and might need more sun.
  • English ivy
    • Pinch to keep bushy and shape. Use the trimming/stem cutting for propagation.
  • Peperomia green bean
    • Clean and prun back in shape when it gets scraggly.
    • Cleaning: use damp towel, take it to shower, allow water to run over the plant, or take it out in the rain.
    • Pruning: when it gets too bushy, prune using clean and sharp scissors. Pruned pieces can be used for propagation.
  • Polka dot plant
    • 4 Feb, x Feb, 12 Feb to 6 cm. Hope will not die out.
    • Cut or pinch back top 2 leaves on each stem weekly, to avoid long, lanky, leggy branches.
    • To extend growing season of the plant, clip flowers as soon as appearing, Because the plant will die off/enter dormancy once it finishes flowering.
  • String of turtles
    • 9 Feb: Currently flowering. Do not prune.
    • Prune regularly so the vining doesn't get too unkempt, leggy, and ragged. Remove dead and damaged stems and leaves, remove unwanted growth. This will encourage new and more vigorous growth.
    • Flowers: If like the appearance, leave them on. If unwanted, prune away to dirct energy for vining growth. When flower is spent, make sure to prune.

Herbs

  • Arugula
    • No need to prune beyond the regular harvesting. But do remove broken or diseased leaves asap.
    • 4-7 weeks after seeding. Wait until it reaches 3" long. Harvest when the leaves are young. Old leaves are tough and bitter.
    • Partial harvest
      • cut just the outer leaves, leaving the crown intact.
    • Full harvest:cut entire leaves just above the ground. If weather is mild, it might regrow.
    • Flower harvest
      • If plant bolts, harvest to eat the flowers but not the leaves, because leaves are turning bitter by now.
    • Use as soon as possible after harvest. Keep in refrigerator up to a week.
  • Basil
    • Harvest and topping a lot. Taste will change throughtout life cycle. Will taste stronger when flowering.
  • Cilantro
    • Mid Feb start harvesting
    • As the young plant grow, pinch back periodically 1" to encourage fuller plants.
    • Ready to harvest in just 3-4 weeks. Cut leaces at the bottom of the plant, avoid harvesting more than 1/3 plant at a time.
    • Coriander seeds harvest can be ready as early as 45 days when the plant is 3-4" tall.
    • To extend harvest, regularly snip soft stems.
  • Kemangi
    • 12 Feb (First pinching, only some), 18 Feb, Mar 4
    • Harvest anytime, harvest frequently. Leave at least a half of the plant. Prune the entire branch except leave few buds.
    • Pinch regularly because the plants tend to get leggy and promote growth.
    • Start when seedling have 3 sets of true leaves: pinch off the top set. Continue this pattern when harvesting, pruning each branch that's harvested to above just its first or second set of leaves.
    • Pinch off flowers for best flavor and extend harvest time. Flowering change the flavor and reduce leaf production. But the flowers aroma does repel many insect pests.
  • Oregano
    • Regularly pinching odd leaves to encourage healthier and bushier plant, increasing harvest too.
    • Can start harvest when plant reaches 4-5 inches tall.
    • Pinch back regularly. Starting when plant 4 inches tall. This will prevent flowering which make the stems woody. Trim back to stimulate growth.
  • Parsley
    • Begin to harvest when it is about 6" tall and relatively bushy. Harvest whole stem from the base to encourage more growth. Take outer leaves, let inner leaves intact to continue to maturity. Do not remove more than 1/3 plants at a time.
  • Rosemary
    • Frequent harvesting and pruning to keep the plant bushy and healthy.
    • Cut stems that's becoming too large and woody. They can be used as skewers.
  • Sage
    • Takes 75 days to reach harvest stage. Harvest minimally at the first year. Harvest before the plants flower for the season for best flavour. Stop harvesting 2 months before first frost.
    • Pick individual leaves by hand, don't take more than ⅓ at a time.
    • Pruning at early spring to delay stem woodiness.
  • Thyme
    • Pinched: 12, 13, 24 Feb
    • Harvest anytime once established. No extra pruning needed when harvested regularly. But it can be cut back if it gets too long/lanky, or to shape. After 3-4 years, potted thyme can be woody. Read.

Flower plants

  • Alyssum
    • Deadhead to keep plants flowering. If it's a large area, can shear by 1/3 instead. Plants tend to be leggy and gangly, prune to keep neat.
  • Bunny Tails
    • Flowers can be dried and last long as bouquets or craft. hang a bunch upside down, dry in a dark, cool, and dry place for 2-3 weeks or until stem snaps when bent. It will last for years
    • Plants sprout easily from seed and baby grasses can be thinned to let stronger plants have room to grow
  • Candytuft
    • After blooming period has ended, prune 1/3 of the top foliage to keep it nice and tidy. Candytuft is typically 1 foot height, but can be pruned to maintain smaller size.
  • Dianthus
    • Removed diseased or damaged branches regularly.
    • Deadhead to prolong flowering, shear to shape after flowering period is finished.
  • Lavender
    • Harvesting flower counts as regular pruning.
    • Spring pruning is recommended to keep the plant well-shaped and to encourage new growth,
    • Prune by cutting back about 5 cm or cut down to the new growth.
    • For harvesting info, check reference.
  • Scarlet Flax
    • prune and cut back flowering stems in early summer to extend bloom period.
  • Soapwort
    • Deadhead flowers to promote continuing flowering.
    • In the fall when the blooming period has ended,cut back about a half to keep it healthy and prevent invasive spreading.

Fruit plants

  • Chili
    • Chili babies: Start pinching top 2 sets of 5: 13 Feb. 18, 24 Feb

So I'm not sure why our plants are about a meter tall or maybe it's not even the same kind. So for the seedling, we are going to follow generic pepper pinching routine. Wait until there are 10 leaves (5 sets), pinch the top 2 sets.

Underground goodies

  • Potato
    • Harvesting young immature potatoes is possible without harm to the plant when the plant reached 1 foot in height, about 50 days after planting. When the plants flower, the are generally ready for harvesting. Gently feel around in the soil and lift out.
    • For full harvest, wait until the plants reach full size and die out. About 2-4 months, up to 120 days. Potatoes can be left in ground at this point for several weeks as long as the ground is not wet.
  • Turmeric
    • No pruning

Trees

  • Ellwood cypress
    • Low maintenance, no pruning required unless bonsai trees. For shaping, best to do it on fall. Remove dead limbs, trim back to shape, but don't cut into old wood. Limbs cut back too far will not generate new growth and will create a bare spot. Gentle pruning to remove unattractive brown dead foliage can be done with blasting water from a hose. Enough to remove dead foliage, without worry cuting to deep. For bonsai plant, pinching with hand regularly to shape is a good idea.

Soil

Rich

  • Poinsettia
    • Well draining peat bsed potting soil.
  • Polka dot plant
    • Rich and well-drained. All purpose potting mix is good. Mix some pumice or perlite to improve drainage
  • String of turtles
    • Mix consisting of organic matter like peat is ideal. Well-draining. Seed starting mix is a good choice. Acidity in peat is good for the plant, but take care that the pH doesn't get too low, check by testing.
  • Turmeric
    • Rich, well drained, consistently moist

Loamy

  • Arugula
    • Loamy rich in nutrient is prefered even though okay with a wide variety. Moist, well-drained, pH slightly acidic to neutral. Likes nitrogen rich soil, consider mix compost before planting.
  • English ivy
    • Best in average loam, well draining. Even though will grow in poor soil too of wide range of pH. Use mulch in dry climate. For indoor, does best in potting mix that is loose and well-drained.
  • Parsley
    • Loamy well-draining soil rich of organic matter. pH slightly acidic to neutral.
  • Potato
    • Transplanted and hilled: 22 Feb
    • Loamy, acidic (5-6). Prone to scab at higher pH. Do not like rich soil. Loose and well-draining. Mulch.
  • Sage
  • Sandy or loamy soil, well-draining. Wet soil can cause rot and can kill the plant. pH: slightly acidic to neutral.
  • Scarlet Flax
    • Any soil type, as long as well draining. Prefers sandy, loamy, or clay soil. Doesn't do well in rich soil.

General well-draining soil

  • African violet
    • Well-draining soil, likes slightly underpotted.
  • Alyssum
    • Moist soil, well-draining. Tolerant of planting location, including sandy beaches and dunes, even wall cracks. Do not like boggy soil with poor drainage.
  • Chili
    • All purpose commercial potting soil. pH range 5.5-7.0.
  • Dianthus
    • Neutral to alkaline, well-drained soil.
  • Ellwood cypress
    • Average, moist, well-drained soil or potting mix.
  • Kemangi
    • Not particular to soil type or pH, but like it well-draining.
  • Peperomia green bean
    • Well-drain soil with moderate water retention capacity. Best in potting mix with 50:50 peat moss and perlite. Cover with worm compost soil.
  • Soapwort
  • well-draining. Might be growing uncontrollably and look disheveled in moist rich soil. Slightly rockier help mild it up.

Sandy / gravelly

  • Bunny tails
    • Like sandy soil most, but any well-draining soil is fine.
    • Candytuft
      • gravelly soil like in their native habitat. Critical to allow excellent drainage. pH more of the alkaline side.
  • Cilantro
    • Ordinaly potting mix is good. Likes airy, light, fast-draining soil with plenty of perlite or sharp sand mixed to inscrease drainage. Use rather potting mix that garden soil which is too heavy. Do not repot. It might bolt at slightest provocations.
  • Haworthia
    • Likes sandy and gravelly soil with excellent drainage.
  • Oregano
    • Regular potting mix blended with coarse sand, finely ground granite, or other material to improve drainage. Airy, light, fast-draining.
  • Rosemary
    • Dry well draining soil. Sandy cactus soil blend works best. Can try mix potting mix a=with sand.
  • Thyme
    • Very dry and well-draining. Sandy mixture is the best. If potting mix is used, mix it with sand or gravel. Thyme is particularly susceptible to root rot and over-watering. Wide range of pH: 6-8.

Lean soil

  • Lavender
    • Lean soil encourage higher concentration of oil (and therefore good smell). Well drained soil in the drier side. If using potting mix, mix it with sand. Alkaline/especially chalky soil enhance fragrance. pH under 6.5 can shorten its life.

Encouraging bloom

  • African violet
    • Sun - Even though direct sun can burn the leaves, it does need some light to encourage blooming. Maybe put the pot in east side facing window, especially if there's a sheer curtain in between. Then it does need 8 hours of darkness at least.
    • Water - African violets prefer to stay evenly moist. Ideally the soil feels like a well wrung-out sponge.
    • Fertilizer - get proper African violet specific vertilizer.
    • Repotting problem - doesn't like to be repotted to a large pot. Prefer to have it rather small. The pot should not be larger than 1/3 of plant's diameter.
    • 6-8 hours of sunlight. Make sure removing shadow. Chase around the light.
    • Pruning each spring for more frequent and fuller bloom. Lavender sets buds on new growth, so stimulating the process can be a signal to the plant.
    • Might not bloom until second or third season
  • Lavender
      Do not fertilize. Fertilizer promotes green growth. If the soil is to fertile, mix it with sand or gravel.

Pest

Fungus gnats

  • Autumn 2021
    • We started seeing the appearance in our indoor plants around the summer 2021. By the time we were leaving for our summer camping trip, July-August, it got pretty bad. Dad treated it when we were away, but it's very hard to make it gone. Late summer to early winter, it's hard to grow seedlings from seeds. Pots after pots, almost none grow. When some do, only very little. Imagine a pot has 2x7 or 2x9 holes, each with several seeds. Usually many seedlings come out from each hole, this time could be just 1 or 2 seedlings from the whole pot. Back then we didn't know what's wrong.
  • Strategies started on late Nov / early Dec 2021:
    • Changing soil
    • Improving drainage by bottom layer pebbles
    • Covering soil top with pebbles or clay beads
    • Less watering
  • Nov 2021
    • Attempt to remove fungus gnats population, especally with renewing soil, reducing the water, and adding sand layer to the surface, was always ongoing. However they just reduced in number and even with a small number of them, a flare up can happen fast.
  • Dec 2021
    • It's not until mid Dec the attempt feels to be taking a long term effect. A new strategy here is covering the soil, especially the one with seeds and seedlings, with cling wrap or paper towel. New soil is used. And following that, even washed seedlings that was previously kept around, was discarded because worry larvae might be actually intact in the roots and can repopulate. With exception of basil and potato that's washed really thoroughly before and after kept in water medium overnight.
  • Jan 2022
    • Since then, gnats population decreased tremendously. Seeds grown in such condition, each holes thriving with abundance of seedlings. by January, almost no gnat seen.
  • Feb 2022
    • A few small outbreaks happen now and then but more or less controlled. Not everyday we see any gnats, and when it does usually just 1 or 2 close to the pot.
  • Strategy started Feb 2021:
    • Deep watering
    • Vegetable safe insecticide spray
      We previously owned marigold based insecticide spray and used it late spring, however we haven't been able to locate the bottle, so we got a new one recently. That's why it's very delayed.
  • Feb 2022 week 1 - Chili
    • In large chili pot, under the fabric covering, there were about 5-8 gnats emerges from the ground. Aggresively dealt with vegetable safe insecticide spray, just around the soil.
